Lec 5Personas, Scenarios & Use Cases

These three artifacts share the same goal — clarity about users and interactions — but each uses a different lens.

PersonaA fictional person grounded in research. Answers: "Who are we designing for?" ScenarioA short narrative with context, trigger, and journey. Answers: "What happens in real life?" Use CaseActor ↔ system interaction with main and alternative flows. Answers: "What must the system do?"

Key idea: These artifacts are "compression." They turn complex humans into a shared reference that supports decisions and makes assumptions visible.

A persona is a fictional character built from research patterns — not stereotypes or assumptions.

What to include (anatomy)
  • Goals — what "success" means to this person
  • Tasks / jobs-to-be-done
  • Behaviours + habits
  • Context of use (where/when)
  • Constraints (time, money, access)
  • Pain points + workarounds
  • Tech proficiency
  • A representative quote (from evidence)
Persona Types
PrimaryMain target. If you satisfy them, you're winning. SecondaryImportant, but mostly satisfied by the primary design. CustomerBuyer/decider. May not be the daily user. ServedAffected by the system even if they don't use it directly. NegativeExplicitly NOT designing for — helps avoid scope creep.

"Personas are not about opinion, hearsay, or stereotypes. They are grounded in evidence."

Scenario
Narrative-based with context and motivation. Short story: context + trigger + journey + outcome. Can be typical, best case, or worst case.
Use Case
Structured actor ↔ system interaction, step by step. Includes precondition, trigger, steps, postcondition, and alternative flows.

Personas compress who; scenarios show context; use cases define system interaction.

Lec 6Requirements & Task Analysis
GoalThe outcome the user wants to achieve. ("Organization picks up correct amounts.") TaskObservable actions with a clear start and end. ("Verify quantities against daily limits.") FeatureA possible system capability that supports tasks. ("A checklist to verify limits.")
What task analysis captures
  • Goal: what "done" means
  • Steps: user actions + system support
  • Decision points and branches
  • Exceptions and workarounds
  • Outputs: what is recorded/produced

A requirement describes a needed capability, condition, or quality. Good requirements are testable — you can check if they're met.

Three Types
FunctionalWhat the system must do. ("System must record an incoming pickup request.") Data/InfoWhat info is captured, stored, or shown. ConstraintsUsability + context constraints. ("Supports low-tech users.")
Good Requirement Checklist
  • Clear and unambiguous — only one interpretation
  • Testable / verifiable — a TA can check it
  • Singular — one requirement = one idea
  • Feasible — matches real constraints
  • Traceable — links to a scenario/task

Use "System must…" for must-haves. Use "System should…" for quality goals. Avoid: "should be easy" without defining "easy."

Lec 8Information Architecture (IA)

IA is a design discipline focused on making information findable and understandable. It covers: organization + navigation + search + labeling of content.

Design for Finding — Information Needs
Perfect catchKnow exactly what you want and where to find it. Lobster trapWant something specific but don't know where it is. Drift netLooking for anything useful, broad exploration. Berry pickingSearch evolves — you pick up info as you go and change direction. Pearl growingStart with one known item, then find related content around it.
The Three Circles of IA

Good IA exists at the intersection of: Users (needs, behaviours), Content (topics, format, structure), and Context (business goals, resources).

Lec 9Card Sorting

Card sorting reveals users' mental models for grouping and labeling. It is primarily used to understand how users categorize and label information to inform IA and navigation.

Open Card Sort
Participants group cards AND create their own category labels. Used when building IA from scratch. Reveals how users think about content.
Closed Card Sort
Categories are pre-defined; participants sort cards into existing groups. Used to test whether existing navigation makes sense to users.
#1Visibility of system status — Keep users informed through timely feedback (loading bars, confirmations). #2Match real world — Use familiar language and conventions. #3User control & freedom — Support undo/redo; provide emergency exit. #4Consistency & standards — Follow platform conventions. #5Error prevention — Design to prevent problems before they occur. #6Recognition over recall — Make options visible; don't force users to remember. #7Flexibility & efficiency — Accelerators for expert users. #8Aesthetic & minimalist design — No irrelevant information; reduce noise. #9Recognize, diagnose, recover from errors — Plain language error messages. #10Help & documentation — Easy to search; focused on tasks.
Lec 10Gestalt Principles + IxD

Gestalt principles explain how humans organize visual information. Designers use them to create layouts that are aesthetically pleasing and easy to understand.

ProximityThings placed close together are perceived as related. Foundation for grouping labels with their fields. SimilarityElements that look alike (same colour, shape, size) are perceived as belonging to the same group. ClosureThe mind fills in gaps to perceive incomplete shapes as whole forms. (A circle of dots = a complete circle.) ContinuationThe eye follows paths and smooth lines, grouping elements that appear to continue a direction. SymmetrySymmetrical elements tend to be perceived as a unified group. Figure/GroundWe separate a foreground element (figure) from its background (ground). Good design ensures content is clearly perceived as figure.

IxD is the creation of a dialogue between a person and a product — physical and emotional, manifest in form, function, and technology over time.

1D — WordsText, button labels — give users the right amount of information. 2D — VisualsGraphical elements: images, typography, icons. 3D — PhysicalMedium of interaction — mouse, touchscreen, fingers. 4D — TimeMedia that changes with time: animations, videos, sounds. 5D — BehaviorHow the previous four dimensions define interactions, and how the product reacts and gives feedback.
Lec 11–12Wireframing + Prototyping
Static Wireframe
Defines layout and content structure. Low fidelity. No interactions. Used for early layout decisions.
Interactive Prototype
Simulates real interactions: clickable flows, transitions. Enables realistic usability testing before development.

An interactive prototype differs from a wireframe in that it simulates real interactions that can be user tested — not just showing layout, but demonstrating behavior.

Lec 15Deceptive (Dark) Patterns

Dark patterns are interface practices that steer, deceive, coerce, or manipulate people into choices that are often not in their best interests (money, data, or attention).

What to look for
  • Unequal choices — easy yes / hard no
  • Hidden or delayed information
  • Ambiguous wording
  • Default settings that benefit the company
  • Hard-to-exit flows (cancel/delete buried)
  • Emotion/urgency pressure

Key idea: The UI is designed so the "best" choice for the company feels like the default for you.

1. Confirmshaming

Uses guilt or shame to make refusal feel like a bad choice. "No thanks, I hate saving money." Neutral option is visually downplayed or insulting. Fair alternative: neutral labels, equal button weight, respectful language.

2. Fake Urgency / FOMO

Creates pressure using countdown timers that reset, or "Only 2 left!" without proof. Leads to impulsive choices. Fair alternative: use verifiable urgency only (real deadlines/stock).

3. Nagging

Repeatedly asks for permission without allowing a durable "No." Only shows "Not now" (never "Never"). Same prompt re-appears each session. Causes consent fatigue. Fair alternative: respect "No" for a meaningful period.

4. Sneaking

Adds extra items, fees, or actions without explicit opt-in. Add-on pre-included in cart; hidden costs appear late in checkout. Fair alternative: default-off for add-ons; clear itemized summary before purchase.

5. Disguised Ads

Makes ads look like navigation, downloads, or normal content. "Download" buttons that are ads. Fair alternative: clear labels ("Ad"), spacing, and visual separation from content.

6. Intentional Misdirection

Uses wording, layout, or visual hierarchy to push toward company-preferred option. Big primary "Accept"; reject is small/hidden. Ambiguous labels like "Cancel." Fair alternative: symmetric prominence for accept vs. refuse.

7. Roach Motel (Easy In, Hard Out)

Signup in 1–2 steps; cancellation buried in settings or requires a phone call. Traps users into subscriptions. Fair alternative: cancellation parity — same channel, comparable steps.

8. Preselection (Defaults)

Pre-checks boxes or toggles so company-benefiting choices happen unless user notices and opts out. Newsletter checkbox already checked. Fair alternative: default-off for non-essential options.

9. Friend Spam

Obtains contacts access and sends invites on users' behalf without full understanding. Privacy invasion; reputational harm. Fair alternative: explicit permission + preview + select recipients.

10. Negative Option Billing / Forced Continuity

A "free trial" quietly converts into recurring charges unless cancelled in time. No reminder before billing; cancellation is hard. Fair alternative: clear renewal terms + reminders + easy cancel via same channel.

Lec 16UX Laws & Cognition

Predicts how long it takes to point/click/tap a target.

MT = a + b · log₂(D/W + 1)
DDistance to target WWidth of target along movement axis IDIndex of Difficulty = log₂(D/W + 1)
Design Tips
  • Tip 1: Make small targets larger — biggest gains come from increasing very small targets.
  • Tip 2: Use screen edges — infinite width at boundaries means faster acquisition (e.g., macOS menu bar at top edge).
  • Tip 3: Optimize distance — place frequent actions closer to where the user's pointer naturally rests.

Decision time increases with the number of choices (and their complexity). More choices → more decision fatigue.

T = a + b · log₂(n + 1)
nNumber of choices TDecision time
Design Tips
  • Simplify navigation across the journey
  • Group related information
  • Prioritize important actions at all times
  • Use progressive disclosure — reveal options only when needed

The average person can only keep 7 (±2) items in their working memory at once. A "chunk" is a meaningful grouping (e.g., 416-555-1212 is 3 chunks, not 10 digits).

Design implication: don't show more than ~7 navigation items; chunk information meaningfully; use grouping to reduce cognitive load.

Users spend most of their time on other sites. They prefer your site to work the same way as those sites.

  • Conventions reduce learning cost (navigation, icons, forms, checkout)
  • Breaking conventions requires a strong payoff + clear signposting
  • In dark patterns, breaking conventions is used to trick (e.g., "X" doesn't close the dialog)

The same human factors that enable great UX can also be exploited as dark patterns — Jakob's Law shows both sides.

Lec 17WCAG Accessibility

WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) has 4 principles and 13 guidelines. The principles spell POUR.

PerceivableInfo must be presented in ways all users can perceive — including those with visual/auditory limitations. OperableUsers must be able to navigate and interact with all elements — including keyboard-only users. UnderstandableContent must be clear and easy to comprehend — predictable, consistent, helpful errors. RobustContent must work across devices, browsers, and assistive technologies now and in the future.
Conformance Levels
Level ABasic accessibility — removes most serious barriers (e.g., alt text for images). Level AAWidely expected practical standard — required by most legal frameworks. (e.g., 4.5:1 colour contrast) Level AAAHighest level — difficult to achieve everywhere (stricter contrast, simpler language).
Perceivable
1.1Text Alternatives — Provide alt text for all non-text content (images, video, audio). 1.2Time-based Media — Captions for video; transcripts for audio. 1.3Adaptable — Content must be presentable in different ways without losing info or structure. 1.4Distinguishable — Make it easy to see and hear content; sufficient contrast (4.5:1 AA for normal text). Formula: L = 0.2126R + 0.7152G + 0.0722B
Operable
2.1Keyboard Accessible — All functionality must be available from a keyboard. 2.2Enough Time — Provide options to extend time limits; no auto-advancing content without pause. 2.3Seizures & Physical Reactions — No content that flashes more than 3 times per second. 2.4Navigable — Help users navigate, find content, and know where they are. 2.5Input Modalities — Make functionality accessible via pointer inputs (mouse, finger, stylus).
Understandable
3.1Readable — Text must be readable and understandable. Use plain language. 3.2Predictable — Pages must appear and operate in predictable ways. 3.3Input Assistance — Help users avoid and correct mistakes with helpful error messages.
Robust
4.1Compatible — Maximize compatibility with current and future user agents and assistive technologies. Use semantic HTML.
Contrast Ratio = (L1 + 0.05) / (L2 + 0.05)

Where L1 = lighter color's relative luminance, L2 = darker color's relative luminance.

L = 0.2126R + 0.7152G + 0.0722B

Lec 19HCI Research Methods
Qualitative
Words, stories, observations. Understand experiences, motivations, context. Smaller samples. Discovers issues. Output: themes, design insights.
Quantitative
Numbers, measures, comparisons. Measure performance and patterns. Larger samples. Compares designs. Output: metrics, graphs.

Qualitative and quantitative methods are not rivals — they answer different questions. Mixed methods are often strongest: qual explains WHY, quant shows HOW MUCH.

When to Use Which
Qual"What frustrates users?" / "What do users need?" Quant"Which design performs better?" / "How many users succeed?" Mixed"What is happening AND why?"
Method 1 — Interviews

A conversation with users about experiences, habits, needs. Semi-structured: some planned questions + room to follow interesting ideas. Good for: exploring a new problem, understanding motivations, finding unmet needs. Watch out for: leading questions, users simplifying what they do.

Method 2 — Observation / Contextual Inquiry

Watching users do real tasks in context. Focuses on what they actually do, not just what they say they do. Reveals hidden workarounds and pain points. Limitation: people may act differently when observed.

Method 3 — Think-Aloud Usability Testing

Give participants tasks; ask them to narrate their thinking. Observe where expectations break down. Produces qualitative insight from comments AND sometimes simple quantitative metrics (success rate, time on task). Good for: finding usability issues, comparing prototype versions.

Method 4 — Surveys

Collect input efficiently from many people. Quantitative side: Likert scales, ratings, rankings. Qualitative side: open-ended responses. Good survey practices: short, one-idea-per-question, balanced response options, avoid double-barrelled or leading questions.

Method 5 — Metrics / Quantitative Evaluation

Common HCI metrics: task completion rate, time on task, error rate, number of clicks, satisfaction rating. A/B testing: compare two interface versions with a measurable outcome. Numbers show WHAT happened; qual tells you WHY.

Formative
Earlier in the process. Aims to improve the design. Examples: interviews, observation, pilot testing.
Summative
Later when design is stable. Aims to assess or compare. Examples: benchmarking, comparison tests, performance metrics.
Lec 20HCI Data Analysis
NominalCategories with no order. (Button colour chosen.) → Use: Mode, Chi-square. OrdinalOrdered but intervals unequal. (Likert scale.) → Use: Median, Mann-Whitney. IntervalEqual intervals, no true zero. (Temperature, IQ.) → Use: Mean, T-test, ANOVA. RatioEqual intervals with true zero. (Task completion time.) → Use: Mean, T-test, ANOVA.

Choosing the wrong test for your data type invalidates your results.

Tests whether the means of two groups differ more than expected by chance. Considers: (1) how big is the difference between group means? (2) how much natural variation exists within each group?

Independent T-test
Two separate groups. (Design A users vs Design B users.) Use when different people are in each condition.
Paired T-test
Same participants, two conditions. (Before/after redesign.) Controls for individual differences; more sensitive.
Assumptions
  • Normality (Shapiro-Wilk test)
  • Equal variance (Levene's test)
  • Independence
p-Value
Probability of results if there were no real effect. p < 0.05 = statistically significant. A threshold, not a measure of importance.
Effect Size
How large is the difference practically? Cohen's d: 0.2=small, 0.5=medium, 0.8=large. Eta-squared η²: 0.01=small, 0.06=medium, 0.14=large.

A p-value without an effect size is an incomplete story. p < 0.05 does not mean the effect is large or practically important.

Use when comparing 3 or more independent groups on one continuous outcome. Example: comparing error rates across 3 navigation designs.

F = variance between groups / variance within groups

A significant F tells you "something differs" — not WHICH pair. Always follow up with Tukey HSD post-hoc test to find which specific pairs differ.

Why not just run multiple T-tests?

Running multiple T-tests inflates the false-positive rate. ANOVA controls for this.

Thematic Analysis (6 Steps)
  • 1. Familiarise — Read all transcripts/notes multiple times; jot initial impressions.
  • 2. Generate Codes — Label meaningful segments; descriptive, close to data.
  • 3. Search for Themes — Group related codes; look for patterns across participants.
  • 4. Review Themes — Check themes against coded data; merge/split as needed.
  • 5. Define & Name — Write a clear definition for each theme.
  • 6. Write Up — Weave themes into a narrative supported by participant quotes.
Affinity Diagramming

Collaborative method to organize observations from interviews, usability tests, or notes into clusters of related ideas.

  • Write one observation per sticky note
  • Silently group similar notes together
  • Label each cluster with a heading
  • Identify higher-level themes from clusters

Digital tools: Miro, FigJam. In-person: physical sticky notes. Both work equally well.

Lec 21AI, No-Code & the Future of UX
No-Code
Visual drag-and-drop; zero programming required. Examples: Webflow, Framer, Bubble, Glide. Users: designers, marketers, non-technical teams.
Low-Code
Visual tools + optional custom code. Examples: Retool, OutSystems, Microsoft Power Apps, Appsmith. Users: developers, technical designers.
Why These Tools Matter for UX
  • Speed: A prototype that took a week can exist in an afternoon.
  • Democratised making: Non-designers can now build functional prototypes, changing the UX designer's role toward evaluator and critic.
  • Cheaper iteration: Explore more directions; throw away bad ideas faster.
  • Lower entry barrier: Non-profits, small businesses can now build their own tools.
Risk 1Generic Design — AI outputs are competent but visually indistinct. Layout reflects no user's actual mental model. Risk 2Poor User Fit — AI defaults to the "average" training-data user (young, English-speaking, tech-literate, Western). Any deviation requires real user research. Risk 3Accessibility Gaps — AI generates visually appealing layouts but misses contrast failures, missing focus indicators, unlabelled buttons, undefined heading hierarchy. Risk 4False Confidence — A hi-fi AI prototype looks "done." Stakeholders skip testing. Fidelity signals completion. Risk 5Embedded Bias — AI-generated personas default to young, able-bodied, tech-literate, Western users. Generated imagery has default assumptions. Designers are responsible.
Outsourced Thinking→ Virtue: Ownership. Think first — bring AI as partner after. If AI disappeared, could you still do your job? Wasted Time→ Virtue: Automation. AI saves time on repetitive tasks. For one-offs, doing it yourself is often faster. Lost Details→ Virtue: Selectivity. AI summaries lose nuance and outliers. When accountable for details, read the raw material yourself. Isolated Ideation→ Virtue: Inclusion. AI ideas don't build team buy-in. Use AI to prepare sharper conversations, not replace them. Naïve Trust→ Virtue: Skepticism. Hallucinations are real. Verify facts, stats, UX guidance. "ChatGPT said" is not evidence. Bland Taste→ Virtue: Originality. AI outputs are competent and generic. Taste and discernment are your differentiating value. Defensive Outlook→ Virtue: Experimentation. Don't resist AI out of pride. Find bottlenecks; give AI a chance there.

From Kate Moran, NN/g. Structure for crafting effective AI prompts.

C — ContextDescribe your situation: your role, the product, the users, the specific task. Brief AI like a new consultant. A — AskRequest a specific action: role you want AI to play, exact output needed, how many options, what format. Be precise. R — RulesProvide constraints: guardrails, best practices, brand voice, character limits, tone requirements. E — ExamplesShow good/bad examples, or existing copy to improve. Providing examples produces more natural output than describing tone.

Vague prompts produce vague outputs. The CARE framework injects your UX knowledge — which the AI doesn't have by default.

AI Genuinely Helps With
  • Planning: Draft study plans, screeners, interview guides
  • Desk research: Aggregate info, summarize reports
  • Analysis: Cluster themes, identify patterns across sessions
  • Ideation: Overcome blank-page syndrome; generate quantity
  • UX writing: Microcopy, error messages, onboarding copy drafts
AI Cannot Replace
  • Real user interviews — the non-verbal layer (body language, hesitation, tone) is irreplaceable
  • Outsourced analysis — you lose nuance, outliers, and surprises
  • Usability testing with real humans — "you will always learn something that changes the design"
  • Judgment about which design option is right for your users, business, and ethics
  • 42% of UX tasks require direct human interaction; 20% are too complex for AI

"Handing entire UX workflows over to AI has not proved productive. The best practitioners think of themselves as strategists who leverage AI to increase what they can accomplish." — NN/g

Lec 22Smallville Paper — 2 exam questions

The Smallville paper (Park et al., 2023) places 25 AI agents in a simulated social environment (a small town called Smallville). The agents exhibit emergent social behaviours — without being explicitly programmed to do so.

Key Concepts
Generative agentsAI agents powered by large language models that can simulate believable human behaviour over time. Emergent behaviourSocial patterns arising spontaneously from agent interactions — not programmed in, but arising from the architecture. Memory streamEach agent maintains a record of experiences, which gets retrieved and used to influence future decisions. ReflectionAgents synthesize memories into higher-level observations ("I'm the kind of person who..."). Enables longer-term planning. PlanningAgents convert reflections into action plans across time (daily schedules, multi-step goals).
Why It Matters for HCI
  • Demonstrates AI can simulate social dynamics — relevant to future UX tools like "synthetic users"
  • Shows emergent social behaviours (a Valentine's party started from one agent's idea, spread across agents)
  • Illustrates both the potential and limits of AI-simulated human behaviour in UX research
  • Agents are not real users — accuracy drops for non-Western, marginalized, or low-income populations

The core claim: Generative agents that combine LLMs with dynamic memory and planning can produce convincingly human-like social behaviour — but they always reflect the biases and averages of their training data.

2 guaranteed questions MCQ or T/F
What the Smallville paper is primarily about

AI agents in a simulated social environment exhibiting emergent social behaviours. NOT: card sorting, WCAG compliance, rural populations, no-code tools.

Key Terms to Know
  • Generative agents — LLM-powered agents that simulate believable human behaviour
  • Emergent social behaviour — arising from interactions, not programmed in
  • Memory stream + reflection + planning — the three-part architecture
  • Synthetic users — AI-simulated participants for UX research (limitation: biased toward average training data)
  • The paper is relevant to: AI in HCI, future of UX research, limits of AI-simulated user behaviour

Tip: If a question asks "The Smallville paper is primarily about…" the answer will involve AI agents, simulated social environments, and emergent behaviour.
